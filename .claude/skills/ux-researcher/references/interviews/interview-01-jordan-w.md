# User Interview 01 — Jordan W.

**Date:** April 7, 2026
**Participant:** Jordan W., 26, graphic designer
**Coffee experience:** 2 years, enthusiast level
**Interview type:** Semi-structured, 45 minutes, Zoom
**Interviewer:** Emily N.

---

## Coffee journey

**Interviewer:** Walk me through how you got into specialty coffee.

**Jordan:** It started during COVID honestly. I was working from home, drinking like four cups of gas station coffee a day, and my roommate was like "dude, try this" and made me a pour-over with some Counter Culture beans. It was like a completely different drink. I didn't even know coffee could taste like fruit.

After that I went down the rabbit hole. Bought a V60, watched every James Hoffmann video, started following roasters on Instagram. Now I'd say I'm intermediate — I know what I like and I can usually get a decent brew, but I still mess up extraction sometimes.

**Interviewer:** What do you struggle with most?

**Jordan:** Consistency. Some days the coffee is amazing and some days the same bean tastes flat and I don't know what I changed. I think my grind is inconsistent — I have a cheap burr grinder. But I'm also not great at identifying what went wrong. Like, is it over-extracted or under-extracted? I know those words but I can't always taste the difference.

---

## Discovery and purchasing

**Interviewer:** How do you find new beans to try?

**Jordan:** Instagram, mostly. I follow maybe 15 roasters and when someone drops something that looks interesting — nice origin story, interesting process, cool bag design honestly — I'll grab it. I also get recommendations from my roommate and from r/coffee.

**Interviewer:** What makes you pick one bean over another?

**Jordan:** Origin is the first thing. I love Ethiopians — natural process especially. If it says "berry" or "tropical fruit" in the tasting notes, I'm buying it. I also look at the roast date — I won't buy anything older than two weeks.

**Interviewer:** Do you look at cupping scores?

**Jordan:** If they're listed, yeah. But most roasters don't put the CQI score on the bag. They write their own tasting notes, which I trust more because they tasted the roasted coffee, not the green. But if I saw a score above 87 I'd be curious. Anything above 90 I'd buy without thinking.

## Current tools and tracking

**Interviewer:** How do you keep track of coffees you've tried?

**Jordan:** *laughs* I don't, really. I take a photo of the bag when I buy it. Sometimes I post it on Instagram stories. But I have no organized system. I've tried spreadsheets but I never keep up with them. I know there are apps but the ones I've tried felt clunky or too serious — like they wanted me to score everything on a 10-point scale and I just want to write "this one tastes like blueberry jam."

**Interviewer:** What would ideal tracking look like for you?

**Jordan:** Quick and casual. Like, I just want to open the app, snap a photo of the bag, write two sentences about how it tasted, and maybe give it a heart or a meh or a no. Three tiers, not ten. And then later I can look back and see all my hearts and go "oh yeah, I should reorder that one."

**Interviewer:** How about the diary feature we showed you?

**Jordan:** The "Want to Try / Tried / Loved" thing? That's actually close to what I want. But the note-taking is too separate from the diary view. I want to see my notes without clicking into each bean. And I'd want a photo attached to each entry — the bag, or my cup that morning. Visual memory matters to me.

---

## Social and community

**Interviewer:** Do you share coffee with friends?

**Jordan:** All the time. Like, literally — I'll brew something amazing and bring my roommate a cup. But also digitally — I screenshot tasting notes and send them to friends. "You need to try this." It would be cool if I could share a bean page from the app and it looked good — like a nice card, not just a URL.

**Interviewer:** Would you use a social feed in a coffee app?

**Jordan:** Maybe? It depends on who's there. If it's random strangers posting reviews I don't care. But if my friends were on it and I could see what they're drinking? That's interesting. Like Letterboxd for coffee. I want to see my friend's diary, not a stranger's.

---

## Wishlist features

**Interviewer:** If you could design any feature, what would you build?

**Jordan:** A "beans like this" recommendation. I find a bean I love and I want the app to say "you'll also love these three." Not just by origin — by flavor profile. If I loved a fruity natural Ethiopian, show me a fruity natural Colombian I haven't tried. Connect the dots for me.

Second thing — brew recipes attached to beans. Not generic pour-over instructions, but "for this specific bean, try 15g at 200°F, 1:16, 30-second bloom." Dial it in for me. Or at least let me save my own recipe when I get it right so I can replicate it.
